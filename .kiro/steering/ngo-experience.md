# NGO experience standards

## Trust architecture

Trust is produced through:

- Specific descriptions of work.
- Clear geographic and population focus.
- Named methodology where approved.
- Honest evidence and publication dates.
- Visible governance, safeguarding, privacy, and contact information.
- Distinguishing current programs from past programs.
- Clear ways to ask questions or report concerns.
- Partner and funder acknowledgment only when verified.

Do not manufacture trust with badges, fake counters, vague claims, or unverified logos.

## Audience routes

The site must support at least these audience intentions:

- A young person or community member trying to understand available support.
- A caregiver, educator, or community leader seeking useful information.
- A partner or funder assessing credibility and fit.
- A policymaker, researcher, or journalist looking for evidence.
- A person considering volunteering, collaborating, or supporting the organization.

The homepage may introduce these routes, but it must not become a portal of equal-sized cards.

## Mental-health content responsibility

- Use person-first, non-stigmatizing language.
- Do not imply all distress is illness.
- Avoid deterministic language and miracle outcomes.
- Do not publish self-harm or suicide content without an approved safeguarding and editorial protocol.
- Do not present generic web copy as clinical guidance.
- Clearly distinguish awareness information, psychosocial support, clinical services, and emergency services.
- Crisis or emergency pathways must be locally verified, dated, and approved before publication.
- Youth stories require consent, privacy review, and safeguarding review.

## Calls to action

Calls to action must match real organizational capacity. Never add “Get Help,” “Donate,” “Join a Program,” or “Volunteer” merely because NGO websites usually contain them.

When a pathway is not confirmed, keep it in the content-gap register rather than publishing a dead or misleading action.
